In 1918, Hoch and her partner, the artist Raoul Hausmann (see "Mueck is Dead", B 68/5) spent their summer vacation on the Baltic island of Wolin. In their accommodation, they found a military memorial table that inspired them to create their own Dadaist absurd paintings (see Hannah Hoch. It all started with Dada!, exhibition catalogue. Berlin Gallery, 2007, p. 165, IL p. 10). After returning to Berlin, they created the first Dada photomontage. Hausmann "consciously proposed to achieve a new creation by cutting, reshaping or demoulding, omitting or adding, thus completely alienating the shape, color and content," Hoch later recalled. She wanted something similar to her large collages. "Only I did this visually through a large number of photos, forming groups according to the content - making a statement" (quoted in ibid., p. 165). Protagonists from the fields of politics, science and culture were depicted. In the upper part of the picture, technological progress and militant politics come together, represented by Albert Einstein and the last German emperor, Wilhelm II. In the lower part, Hoch sees Dadaism as a mass movement, always striving to carve up the recognized authorities. With alarming frequency, the artist stages ironic gender swaps, with Paul von Hindenburg as a belly dancer and George Grosz as a ballerina. It is a complete photo-disassembly, with which Hoch responds to her time.
